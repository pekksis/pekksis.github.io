The Core Game Loop

The game is divided into two repeating phases. Adventures are hired missions into the uncharted continent. Downtime is the recovery, preparation, and growth between them. Every cycle begins and ends at Havenport, the last outpost of civilisation before the wilds.

Downtime

~2 weeks real time · months in-game
  • Characters recover from injuries sustained on the last adventure and advance skills and purchase new talents
  • Characters perform various downtime activities
  • GMs post expeditions; players post looking-for-group notices
  • Party and GM agree on a schedule and begin

Adventure

~1 month real time · days in-game
  • One or more sessions with the same party and GM
  • Explore the continent, complete the mission, survive
  • Ends when all players return to Havenport

Advancement

Skills improve during Downtime. Your choices during an adventure unlock Talents — permanent abilities that make your character more effective both in and out of combat. Talents are the primary way characters grow in power and identity over time.

The Skill Roll

When you attempt something with a real chance of failure, the GM calls for a Skill Roll. Build your dice pool, roll everything at once, and count successes.

Step 1 — Build Your Pool

  • Attribute Die — reflects raw capability, set by the skill's linked attribute. Ranges from d12 (weakest) to 2d6 (godly). Rolls use two dice, either from the same attribute or a combination of two.
  • 2 Fate Dice. Default: d10 each.

Step 2 — Count Successes

This is a roll-under system. Smaller numbers win, and smaller dice are better. Any die at or below your Skill Level counts as one success. Meet the required number and you succeed. A natural 1 counts as two successes.

Your character must leap a crumbling stone bridge. The GM rules a SWIFT (d8) check against Athletics, with 2 successes required to clear the gap and one success to make it across with a cost.

You roll your two d8s and two d10s, getting 5, 3, 5, and 7. Since your Athletics Skill Level is 4, only the 3 counts as a success. 1 success (2 needed). The GM rules that you make it across, but your sword clatters down into the chasm.

Advantage & Disadvantage

Favourable conditions may earn Advantage, shrinking Fate dice to d8. Unfavourable conditions bring Disadvantage, inflating them to d12.

Helping & Group Rolls

One character usually rolls for the group; others may add their Attribute die if they can justify helping. When survival is individual, such as crossing a chasm, or staying afloat in a vortex, everyone rolls and failure is personal.

Skill Advancement

Roll a natural 1 on any skill check to earn Skill XP. Enough XP and the skill advances one level.

Combat

Combat uses the same roll-under mechanic, but with a fixed Target Number of 4 for both players and enemies. Roll your two weapon Attribute dice plus two Fate dice and count results at or below the Target as successes.

Actions

On your turn you can take one Slow and one Fast action, or two Fast actions. Helping another character costs one action of the same speed — declared before any dice are rolled.

Slow Actions

  • Attack
  • Cast a spell
  • Flee

Fast Actions

  • Manoeuvre (Move, Rise, Withdraw)
  • Ready weapon (Draw, Reload)
  • Aim
  • Use item

Outside your turn you may also Contest or Avoid incoming attacks — see below.

Contest vs Avoid

When attacked, declare your response before any dice are rolled.

Contest

Weapon or shield meets the blow. Both sides roll. Your successes cancel the attacker's — and if you come out ahead, you deal damage and may apply combat effects right back. Requires a weapon capable of parrying, or a shield. Once per round.

Avoid

You get clear of the attack. Your successes cancel the attacker's, but you deal no damage back and cannot apply effects. Unlimited uses, but each Avoid after the first is made at a penalty. Unless you are wielding a shield, you must have a square where you can retreat to.

Contest Outcomes

Armor

Armor reduces damage from the incoming hit. Armor has three tiers; Light, Medium, and Heavy. Heavier armor reduces more damage but makes you less agile, making your Avoid rolls and some skill rolls more difficult.

Combat Effects

When your net successes earn a combat effect, choose one from the options below. Effects apply immediately after damage is resolved.

Shove

Drive your opponent back a short distance in a direction you choose.

Grapple

Seize and restrain your opponent. A grappled target cannot move and suffers Disadvantage on attacks that don't target you. Either party may attempt to break free on their turn.

Manoeuvre

Reposition during the exchange — rise from prone, withdraw from reach, or shift to a better position.

Compel Surrender ✦

Attempt to force the surrender of a cornered or disadvantaged opponent — one who is disarmed, prone, gravely wounded, or otherwise at your mercy. No damage is dealt. Cannot be used on mindless creatures.

Major Combat Effects — requires Att +3

Bypass Armour

Your strike finds a gap in the target's defences. Ignore their armour value entirely for this hit.

Disarm

Knock, wrench, or cut the weapon from your opponent's grip. They must spend a Fast action to retrieve it.

Talents unlock additional combat options. As your character advances, talents may grant access to new effects and techniques unavailable to untrained fighters.

Wounds & Injuries

Every character can absorb Wounds equal to their Toughness. Once their Wounds exceed their Toughness, they suffer an Injury and Wounds reset to 0. A single hit dealing more than your Toughness in damage inflicts 2 Injuries instead.

Taking Damage

Wounds ≤ ToughnessMark the wounds.
Wounds exceed Toughness1 Injury. Wounds reset to 0.
Damage > Toughness2 Injuries. Wounds reset to 0.
Damage > 2× Toughness2 Injuries and immediately at Death's Door.

Injuries

Each Injury sends you to the Injury Table. Effects — such as a lost reaction or an Attribute die inflated to d20 — persist until the adventure ends. They cannot be healed mid-adventure except by rare magic. Wounds can be treated between fights.
